How did it start?

Ice Hockey is thought to have began in the 18th and 19th centuries within Britain and Ireland, evolving from simple stick and ball games. The North American sport, lacrosse was also thought to have contributed to the development of Ice Hockey. The first recorded public indoor Ice Hockey game took place in the Victoria Skating Rink of Montreal in 1875 with rules highly similiar to field hockey. Many of these early games had teams from universities and were often very violent. The game of Ice Hockey has been refined over the many years, developing more and more rules to keep the game interesting and humane.


Victoria Rink in 1893

In the early games of undeveloped Ice Hockey, the goaltender did not have any specialised equipment. Leg pads were eventually adopted in order to protect the goaltenders legs. The earliest leg pads evolved from cricket pads which were made of leather and stuffed with deer hair. In the 1930s the leg pads were further developed, becoming wider and thicker. The pads eventually had additional rolls added onto them and synthetic leather paired with high density foam started to take over. Around the 2000s, 'box' style leg pads became more popular as the 'butterfly position' became more known and used.


Jacques Plante in goaltending equipment (1940s)

Face protection was not used by goaltenders in a game until 1930 when Clint Benedict wore an American football nose-guard to protect his broken nose. Clint did not continue to wear the mask after his injury. Jacques Plante was the first goaltender to wear a mask 'full-time' and in 1959 he began wearing it outside of practices in a game. The first masks designs were named face huggers as they would cover the entire face and attached directly onto your skin with eye and mouth holes cut in. Small holes were cut into the masks to help with ventilation. Some goalies prefered to wear player helmets which were plastic and had inner foam with a metal bar cage. Danglers (curved sheet of plastic) were sometimes attached to these helmets for additional protection around the clavical area. Nowadays goalie helmets are made of a thick stainless steel cage with a carbon fibre and kevlar shell. The helmet is specifically shaped to deflect and rebound pucks when hit directly in the frontal area.


Goaltender masks of the 80s

Originally, goalies used the same sticks as forwards and defensemen, which were straight and narrow. However, as the role of the goaltender evolved, so did their gear. The first major change came with the introduction of a wider blade in the 1920s, designed to better stop pucks. In the following decades, the curve of the blade was added, providing greater control and accuracy. The material used in goalie sticks also evolved from wood to fibreglass and composite materials, significantly improving durability and performance. Modern goalie sticks are now engineered with specific weight distributions and ergonomic features to enhance a goalies ability to block shots and handle the puck, reflecting the advanced technology and sophisticated design processes behind contemporary hockey equipment.


The techniques of ice hockey goaltenders have evolved dramatically over the years, reflecting changes in the game's pace and the advancement of equipment technology. In the early days, goaltenders primarily relied on a stand-up style, remaining upright and using their stick and skates to block shots. This approach gradually shifted with the development of the butterfly style in the 1990s, where goalies drop to their knees and use their leg pads to cover the lower part of the net, effectively blocking low shots. This style became dominant due to its effectiveness in handling the increasingly powerful and accurate shots from modern players. Additionally, the RVH (Reverse-VH) technique, where a goalie uses the post to seal off the bottom of the net while on one knee, has become a crucial tool for handling sharp-angle shots and wraparounds. Modern goaltenders also emphasise agility, reflexes, and positioning, often training with specialised coaches to enhance these skills. The integration of video analysis and sports science has further refined their techniques, enabling goalies to anticipate plays and react with split-second precision. This evolution in goaltending techniques reflects the continuous adaptation to the fast-paced, high-skill demands of contemporary ice hockey.
